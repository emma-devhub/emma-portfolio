The Evaluation Gap

Vertical AI does not stall because the model is mediocre. It stalls because the domain refuses to tell you when you are right.

Here is the stack I keep coming back to. Chat, or the agent loop, is a new kind of UI, or maybe a new kind of SDK. An MDP, a model-driven decision process, plugs in live data. Skills are domain-specific knowledge. Lay those three on top of each other and, in theory, you really can stand in for a mediocre worker in a specific domain. The model reasons, the data grounds it, the skills tell it how this particular field actually behaves. On a whiteboard it closes cleanly.

In practice, deployment is hard in ways the whiteboard does not predict, and for a while I kept asking myself the same question: what exactly is wrong here? I have circled it from a few directions, and at least one part of the answer now feels solid to me. The problem is not the model. The problem is that there is no evaluation function.

Consider why coding works so well, because the contrast is the whole point. Coding has a closed training loop. Every prediction, meaning every piece of code you write, has a knowable and absolute verdict. Does it run? Is the result what you expected? You do not need anyone's permission or opinion to find out. You execute it, and reality answers immediately. That tight loop between a prediction and its correction is what lets the system improve, and it is what makes the whole "model plus data plus skills" stack feel inevitable when you first sketch it.

Most tasks are not like that. Most tasks have no evaluation function at all. You produce something, and there is no clean, absolute signal that comes back to tell you it was right or wrong. The judgment is delayed, or contested, or simply never arrives. When the loop cannot close, the model has nothing to grade itself against, and no amount of capability fills that hole. So the bottleneck in vertical AI is not intelligence. It is the missing scorecard.

The sharpest way I have found to think about this is to ask, of any task, whether it is closeable or not closeable. This is a better axis than "easy versus hard," because it cuts in a direction that surprises you. Some tasks resist closure for a structural reason rather than a difficulty reason. Take accountability: when a human being must answer for the outcome, must be the one held responsible, the task becomes not closeable almost by definition, because the point of it was never just the answer. It was someone standing behind the answer. No evaluation function reaches that.

But the lens cuts both ways, and that is what makes it honest rather than just pessimistic. Plenty of work that we have wrapped in human judgment is, underneath, perfectly closeable. A/B testing is the clean example. The logic of an A/B test is a closed loop by construction: you ship a variant, you measure, the result is the result. On that kind of task an AI product manager should outperform a human one, not because it is smarter, but because it can run the loop without ego and without fatigue. It will not fall in love with its own hypothesis. It will not get tired of being wrong on the way to being right. Where the loop closes, the human advantage I assumed was permanent turns out to be mostly emotional overhead.

I did not arrive at any of this abstractly. At ChatGeneT, the clinical training simulator I helped build, this was the entire problem, stated plainly. Clinical dialogue gives you no clean ground truth. There is no compiler for a conversation with a simulated patient, no single line you can run to see whether the model's reply was correct. So the real product, the thing we were actually engineering, was never just the model. It was the evaluation and release discipline I built around it: how we decided what was good enough to ship, how we caught the failures the model could not catch on its own, how we kept a contested, open-ended task honest. That discipline is how we held hallucinations to 0.31 percent. Not by hoping for a better base model, but by manufacturing, by hand, the scorecard the domain would not give us.

The instinct behind it is older than that job. On a markets desk you live and die by ground truth. A trade is right or wrong by the close, and the close does not care what your thesis was. You learn very quickly to distrust any process that cannot be scored, because the unscoreable ones are exactly where people fool themselves the longest. Years of that leaves you with a reflex: before I trust a workflow, I want to know how it will be marked, and who or what does the marking. If the answer is "nobody, really," I treat the whole thing as suspect, however clever it looks.

That reflex is, I think, the right one to bring to vertical AI. The interesting question is almost never whether the model is good enough. Models keep getting better on a schedule that has very little to do with my particular domain. The interesting question is whether you can build the evaluation loop that the domain will not give you for free. Can you define what right means here, can you measure it cheaply enough to run often, can you close the loop where the world left it open? Where you can, the stack works, and a mediocre worker really is replaceable. Where you cannot, you do not have a product yet. You have a demo that no one is allowed to grade.

So I have stopped asking whether the model is ready. I ask whether the task is closeable, and if it is not, whether I can make it so. That is the real engineering, and it is the part no base model ships with.
