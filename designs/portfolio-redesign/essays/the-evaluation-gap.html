The Evaluation Gap

Why most vertical AI fails on the scoring function, not the model.

The first thing I learned shipping AI into a real domain is that when an agent underperforms on a task that isn't actually that hard, the model is rarely the bottleneck. The instinct in the room is always to reach for a bigger model, more context, better prompts. Sometimes that helps. More often the agent is doing competent work that nobody can grade, and the absence of grading is the whole problem.

It helps to be precise about why coding agents work as well as they do. People credit the models, and the models are good, but the deeper reason is structural: code has a closed loop. Every output has a knowable, near-absolute right or wrong. It compiles or it doesn't. The tests pass or they fail. The function returns what you expected or it returns something else. That ground truth exists independent of opinion, which means an agent can be scored, corrected, and improved against a signal that doesn't argue back. Closed loops are how systems get better on their own.

Most knowledge work has no such loop. Ask an agent to draft a clinical assessment, write a memo, prioritize a roadmap, or counsel a customer, and there is no compiler. There is no row of green checkmarks. The output might be excellent, mediocre, or quietly wrong, and the domain itself does not hand you a function that tells you which. This is the part the model conversation skips. The hard problem in vertical AI is usually not making the agent capable. It is constructing the evaluation function that the domain refuses to provide.

I think about the stack as three layers. Chat and agents are the new interface, the way you talk to the system and the way it acts. A model-driven decision process lets you plug in live data and state. Skills are the domain-specific knowledge that makes the thing competent in a narrow field. Stack those three and, in principle, you can stand in for a mediocre worker in a specific domain. The theory is clean. Real deployment is full of obstacles, and the question I kept returning to was simply: what, exactly, is wrong? The answer was almost never the model. It was that I had no honest way to know if the output was good.

This is where my time at ChatGeneT taught me more than any benchmark could. We built a patient simulator for clinical training, now used in more than thirty hospitals. Clinical dialogue is exactly the kind of domain that gives you no clean ground truth. A simulated patient can be medically plausible and still wrong in a way that matters, and "wrong" in a teaching context is not a single number. So the product was not really the model. The product was the evaluation and release discipline around it: a taxonomy for the kinds of dialogue we cared about, an eval framework that turned soft clinical judgment into something we could actually measure, and release gates that nothing crossed without passing. That is how we held hallucinations to 0.31 percent. Not by hoping the model behaved, but by building the scoring function the clinical domain would not give us, and refusing to ship past it.

That instinct is older than my time in AI. On a markets desk you live and die by ground truth. A trade is right or wrong by the close, and the P&L does not care about your thesis. You learn to distrust any process that can't be scored, because an unscored process is just a confident opinion compounding in the dark. When I moved into AI products, that was the reflex I carried over: before you trust an output, ask what would tell you it was wrong, and whether that signal exists at all.

The lens also tells you where agents genuinely shouldn't go yet, and it isn't where people assume. The dividing line is whether a task is closeable. Some tasks resist closure for a structural reason: they require a human to be accountable, and accountability is not something you can evaluate your way around. But the same lens cuts the other way. A/B testing is itself a closed loop. The metric is the ground truth, the feedback is honest, and the iteration is mechanical. On that kind of task an AI product manager will eventually outperform a human one, not because it is smarter, but because it can run the loop without ego or fatigue.

So I've stopped asking whether the model is good enough. The model will keep getting better on its own schedule, and that's the part I worry about least. The real engineering question in vertical AI is whether you can build the evaluation loop the domain won't give you for free. The teams that win the hard verticals will be the ones that treat the scoring function as the product, not the afterthought. That is the gap. It is closeable, but only on purpose.
