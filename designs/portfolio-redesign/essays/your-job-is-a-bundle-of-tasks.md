# Your Job Is a Bundle of Tasks

*The wrong question is "Will AI take my job." The right unit of analysis is not the title, but the task.*

I want to start with a confession about framing. When people ask "Will AI take my job," they are usually picturing the job as a single, indivisible thing: a title that either survives or disappears. I think that picture is the source of most of the confusion. I am not an economist, and my goal here is not to hand you a conclusion. It is to give you a way to think about the question, a starting point you can use on your own work, especially if you are early in your career and the uncertainty feels personal.

So let me reframe the unit of analysis. In economics there is a basic but widely used framework called the task model, first proposed by the MIT economist David Autor. The core idea is simple and matches intuition: a job is not an indivisible whole. It is a series of specific tasks. Whatever your profession, your day decomposes into a chain of repeated activities. The job is the bundle; the tasks are the parts.

This is not just a metaphor. Whenever you read research on whether AI will replace human work, one term keeps appearing: O*NET. O*NET is a database that breaks every occupation down into dozens, sometimes hundreds, of concrete task dimensions: the knowledge you need, the skills you use, the specific activities the work involves. Take "lawyer." O*NET does not treat "lawyer" as one solid thing. It treats it as a folder, and inside that folder are many subtasks of very different character: contract review, legal research, courtroom argument, client communication, risk judgment, and so on. The title is just the label on the folder. The substance is the contents.

Once you see your job this way, the question "Will AI take my job" splits into something more tractable. To answer it honestly, you need to know three things.

The first thing is how well AI performs each of your subtasks. If your job is a folder of subtasks, the natural question is how AI does on each one. If it performs poorly across the board, your job is safe. If it performs each subtask very well, you have reason to feel uneasy. This is something more and more people are already feeling at work: on a surprising number of individual subtasks, AI is simply good.

The second thing is whether AI is a substitute or a complement on each task. The answer is not uniform across the folder. On some subtasks AI competes with you and does the work instead of you. On others it does not compete; it clears away the supporting work and makes your own contribution more valuable. Ideally you want the complementary tasks to be the larger share of your job and the substitutable ones to be the smaller share. I will develop this distinction properly in Part Two, so for now I only want to plant it.

The third thing is how the tasks combine. A job is not a simple sum of independent tasks. It is not that you do ten unrelated things each day and your output is just those ten things added together. The relationships between tasks matter. Sometimes they combine additively, sometimes multiplicatively, and that difference changes how a single weak or strong task affects the value of the whole. This, too, I will save for Part Two. Here I only want it on the table: combination structure is the third question.

Those are the three questions. Now let me go one level deeper on the first one, because it has its own variable. When we ask whether AI can automate a given subtask, Autor's framework gives us a way to measure it: the degree to which the task is codifiable. The intuition is easy. Can the task be written down as a clear set of rules? A task with explicit rules, high repetition, and predictable inputs and outputs is highly codifiable. The word itself sounds like programming, and that is no accident. Tasks like this face strong substitution: AI can do them well.

Writing code is the obvious example. Not "being a software engineer," but the narrow act of writing code: clear rules, high repetition, predictable inputs and outputs. AI does it fast, does it well, and does it at a fraction of the human cost. If you measured the work in lines of code produced, this subtask faces strong substitution. AI can do it in your place.

Contrast that with tasks that require reading context, navigating relationships between people, making judgment calls under time pressure, or carrying real responsibility, such as a doctor making a treatment decision where the stakes are high. These tasks are hard to reduce to explicit rules. They are low in codifiability. And a low-codifiability task tends to face the opposite of substitution: complementarity. AI can clear away the surrounding rule-bound work, which makes your judgment on the core task more valuable, not less.

This is also, I think, part of why entry-level positions have thinned out over the last couple of years. The early stages of many careers are built largely from high-codifiability subtasks, exactly the kind AI now does well. It is worth asking yourself honestly: how codifiable is what I do each day? Work that is mostly managing people, by contrast, is harder to codify, because it bends with circumstance and with the specific people involved. That does not make such work immune to AI. It only means codifiability is not the thing that puts it at risk. Why those jobs might still be affected is a separate question, and one for later.

So that is the framework I want to build before we go further. Your job is a bundle of tasks. The unit of analysis is the task, not the title. To judge AI's effect on your work you need three things: how well AI performs each subtask, whether AI substitutes for or complements you on it, and how the tasks combine into the whole. We have settled the first question and its variable, codifiability. In Part Two I want to pick up the second: the difference between complement and substitute, and how to stress-test your own job against it.
