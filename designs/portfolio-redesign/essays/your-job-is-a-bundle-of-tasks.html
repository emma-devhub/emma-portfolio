Your Job Is a Bundle of Tasks

A framework for the only AI-and-work question worth asking about yourself.

"Will AI take my job" is a lazy question, and I say that as someone who gets asked it at dinner parties roughly once a week. It is lazy because it treats a job as one indivisible thing, a single switch that AI either flips or does not. That is not what a job is. A job is a bundle of tasks, priced and assembled by an employer who could in principle unbundle it tomorrow. If you want a useful answer, you have to take the bundle apart and look at the pieces. So here is the framework I actually run, including on myself.

Step one: decompose. Write down what you do as a list of tasks, not a title. A "financial analyst" is not one job. She gathers data, cleans it, builds the model, sanity-checks the output against her intuition, writes the memo, and walks a portfolio manager through the call. Six tasks, at least. The title was always a billing convenience. The tasks are where the economics live.

Step two: complement or substitute, task by task. This is just price theory. For each task, ask whether AI lowers the cost of doing it (substitute) or raises the value of the human doing the rest (complement). Data cleaning: substitute, and a good one. Building the standard model: increasingly substitute. Sanity-checking the output, knowing the number is wrong because it disagrees with what the repo market did last Tuesday: complement, and the model makes that judgment more valuable, not less, because now there is more machine output to catch errors in. Notice that the same person holds both kinds of task. Most people do. That is why the binary question is useless.

Step three: find your O-ring, and distrust the people who tell you about it. The economist Michael Kremer wrote about production where tasks are not additive but multiplicative. One bad O-ring brings down the whole shuttle, no matter how good every other component was. In multiplicative production, the task you do well multiplies the value of everything else in the chain, and the task you do badly destroys it. Find yours: the one task where your quality sets the ceiling on the entire output.

Here is the warning. When a CEO tells you "don't worry, you're the O-ring, AI can't replace judgment like yours," that is a bait-and-switch. It is reassurance offered precisely so you stop asking the next question. The O-ring argument is true as production theory and worthless as job security, because it says nothing about how many O-rings the shuttle needs. Which brings me to the step almost everyone skips.

Step four: demand elasticity. This is the part that separates people who studied economics from people who read one article about it. Suppose AI makes your most substitutable task ten times cheaper. What happens to employment in your role depends entirely on what demand does in response. If cheaper output expands demand by more than ten times, you get more work, not less. If demand barely moves, the same output now needs a tenth of the people, and the role survives while the headcount collapses. High elasticity, you are probably fine. Low elasticity, you are genuinely at risk, and no amount of being the O-ring saves you, because the firm needs fewer O-rings now.

The classic case is the bank teller. ATMs did not eliminate tellers; the cost per branch fell, banks opened far more branches, and teller headcount held for years. High elasticity. Now compare a back-office reconciliation team where the work is capped by the number of trades and nobody wants "more reconciliation." Make it ten times cheaper and you do not get ten times the demand. You get layoffs with the job description still printed on the org chart.

So run it on yourself, honestly. Take my own analyst from step one. Data gathering and cleaning: substitutable, low elasticity, closeable. Standard modeling: substitutable, medium elasticity. The judgment call, the memo that a PM trusts enough to put on risk, the relationship: complementary, and the demand for good judgment rises as machine output floods the zone. That is where I would concentrate, not because the title is safe, but because those tasks are complementary and the demand for them is elastic.

One more test I find clarifying, and it is brutal. If you vanished for a day and a competent colleague could cover your work almost losslessly, that work is easy to replace, by a person or a model. The tasks that are hard to hand off, the ones that carry your specific accumulated context, are the ones with a moat. Replaceability is the real measure, and most people overestimate their own.

I think this way because I was trained to. Before AI I built trading products on a markets desk, where you learn fast that the question is never "is this asset good," it is "good at what price, relative to what." Decompose, price each piece, watch the second-order effects. AI is just another input cost falling, and falling inputs reshape demand in ways that are not intuitive until you draw the curve.

So stop asking whether your job will survive. The job, as a label, almost always survives. Ask instead which of your tasks are closeable, which are complementary, and which sit on demand that actually grows when the work gets cheap. Then move yourself, deliberately, toward the second and third. That is a question you can act on. The other one was only ever good for dinner parties.
