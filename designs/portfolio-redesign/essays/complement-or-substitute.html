Complement or Substitute: How to Stress-Test Your Own Job Against AI

Part two of a two-part essay. Part one built the task model: a job is a bundle of tasks, and what matters is how codifiable each one is. Here I turn that lens on a harder question, and on what you can actually write down about your own work.

In the first part of this essay I argued that a job is not an indivisible whole. It is a bundle of tasks, and the right unit of analysis is the task, not the title. O*NET breaks a single occupation into dozens or even hundreds of task dimensions. A lawyer is not "a lawyer" but a folder containing contract review, legal research, courtroom argument, client communication, risk judgment, and much else. Whether AI threatens you depends on how it performs on each of those tasks, and on how codifiable each one is. That is the supply side of the picture. Now I want to add the parts that I think people most often miss.

For each task, ask: does AI replace you, or raise your value?

The same tool can be a substitute on one task and a complement on another, so you have to answer this task by task. Picture your work as a small factory in which you and AI share a production line, and at the end there is something you deliver. That delivered thing is your value.

When AI is a complement, it does stage A of the line very well and very cheaply, and it does not come for stage B. That frees you to spend more time on B. When AI is a substitute, the thing you are best at is also stage A, and AI now does that same A faster, better, and cheaper. In that case there is no obvious reason to keep you. This is a large part of why junior roles have thinned out over the past two years. The early stage of many careers is built out of highly codifiable tasks, and those are exactly the ones AI takes first. By the same logic, more senior work tends to be management, which is contextual and person-specific and therefore harder to codify. That does not make it safe forever. It just means it is not this step that puts it at risk.

Tasks do not simply add up

You might think you can now score each task for how well AI does it, sum the scores, and read off how replaceable your job is. You cannot, because tasks combine in two different ways. Sometimes the structure is additive: ten independent tasks, and the value of the output is just their sum. But often the structure is multiplicative, and this is what makes real work complex.

Consider a marketing campaign. You run the research, you write the copy, you do a great deal of careful work, and then you decide where and when to place the ad. If the research and the copy are excellent but you place the ad in the wrong channel, the whole project lands without a ripple. Or consider a kitchen. Many people can chop and cook, but a great head chef has a particular judgment about seasoning, about when to add the salt and when the sugar, and that one task is why the dish sells for thousands rather than tens.

That decisive task is the O-ring. The name comes from the rubber seal whose failure destroyed a space shuttle: one small component fails and the entire system fails with it. In a job, the O-ring is the node where doing it well multiplies the value of everything else, and doing it badly wastes everyone else's work. It is the most valuable part of the job.

The O-ring has layered consequences for AI. If your O-ring is something AI cannot do, then even if AI does the other ninety percent of your tasks at full quality, it cannot fully replace you, because you still hold the critical node. That is the good news, and it is the line many CEOs stop at. But there is a second layer that those reassurances skip. If AI does the routine ninety percent, your capacity is amplified: the project that once took you all day now needs only your O-ring time, so you can run three projects instead of one. With demand held constant, the team that needed nine people now needs three. You were not replaced. Your job still exists. But fewer of you are required, and the rest are let go. This is what every technical revolution has actually done, from the steam engine to the internet: the headcount per unit of output falls. And in the worst case, AI eventually does the O-ring too, as is happening with some call-center work, and then the substitution is complete. So the honest question is not whether AI can fully replace a job. It is how fast, and the speed differs by occupation. O-ring density is a buffer, not a moat, because as AI's capabilities expand, their overlap with those critical tasks grows rather than shrinks.

The part most people forget: demand elasticity

Everything above is supply-side analysis. The variable I think matters most is on the demand side. When AI cuts the cost of your service, does the quantity demanded rise to absorb your amplified capacity? A clinic that could see ten patients a day because the doctor spent hours writing notes can, once AI writes the notes, see many more. Demand expands, and the shock barely registers. But if demand for your skill is already saturated and AI takes part of your work, you are exposed, because even if you keep the job you may not keep the pay.

This explains an apparent contradiction. People assume programmers are easy to replace, yet demand for them has been rising and pay has held up. OpenAI's study, which used O*NET data to estimate demand elasticity by occupation, found software developers and designers to be highly elastic, while firefighters, call-center staff, and editors came out as low-elasticity. Cheaper software lets many small firms finally afford development they could not before, so the market expands. Low-elasticity jobs have no such cushion. And none of this is linear. The fear is a sudden, large cost drop that floods supply faster than elasticity can absorb it.

Four questions to write down about your own job

  1. How dense are my O-rings? How much of my work is the kind where doing it well makes me indispensable and doing it badly wastes the whole team's effort? The higher the share, the later AI reaches you.
  2. Which tasks is AI taking, and how deeply? Once it does them well, what do I do with the freed time, and is any of it the company's core-value work? If the work you could grow into is not core value, the role may not be for you.
  3. What is my demand elasticity? If AI makes our product cheaper, does demand expand, or is my industry saturated? If it is low, shifting toward a higher-elasticity industry is a real option.
  4. How fast will my industry actually adopt? Small firms and tech move quickly; traditional finance is slow. As Acemoglu argues, the labor-market shock is non-linear: technology and organizational change mature together, and at some point the new working pattern arrives all at once. Adoption lag only buys you a window, and that window is shrinking for everyone.

The point of all this is not a conclusion. I cannot tell you whether your specific job survives, and I do not know my own answer either. What I can offer is a set of drawers to put your intuitions in, so that you have a starting point for forming your own judgment, and for deliberately designing your career rather than waiting to find out. If you are early in your career, that starting point is the whole gift. Raise your share of O-ring tasks, tilt your work toward complement rather than substitute, move toward elastic demand, and where adoption is still slow, become the person who understands AI best. I run this framework over my own days, and that is the most useful thing I know how to share.
