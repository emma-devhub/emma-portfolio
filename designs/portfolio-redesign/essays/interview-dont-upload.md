# Interview, Don't Upload

*The hard part of building an expert agent is getting what is in someone's head into it, and an upload box asks people to do the one thing they are worst at.*

When people set out to build an agent that captures their expertise, the default plan is almost always the same. Upload your files. Write down your process. The interface assumes that what you know is already sitting somewhere, in a folder or a document, waiting to be pointed at. I have come to think this is the wrong interface, and that the wrongness is not a detail. It is the whole problem in miniature.

The real difficulty in building an expert agent is internalizing a person's knowledge: getting what is in their head into the agent. And the trouble with "upload your files" or "write down your process" is that it asks people to do the exact thing they are worst at. Most people cannot accurately describe what they know. They are far better at answering a question than at laying out their own way of thinking in a clean, ordered form. This is, I think, why so few people can write a book, even though most people can follow the ideas in one perfectly well. Understanding a method and being able to narrate your own method are two different skills, and the second is rare.

So if you ask an expert to sit down and write out everything they know, you will get a thin, self-conscious sketch that leaves out most of what makes them good. Not because they are holding back, but because that is not how the knowledge is stored. The fix is almost embarrassingly simple once you see it. Do not ask people to describe themselves. Interview them. Ask specific questions and let them do the thing they are actually good at, which is answering. The structure can come from the questions. What you need from the person is only their answers.

This reframes what is actually scarce. The bottleneck is not storage or model quality. It is the ability to extract a person's knowledge, ideas, and taste, and to make it manifest in a form an agent can use. In Chinese I reach for 显化, to bring something latent into the open, because the cleaner English word is "distill" and distill barely has a tidy equivalent in Chinese at all. I think that linguistic gap is part of why the problem stays underrated. We do not have an easy name for the skill of pulling a fully formed practice out of a head that has never had to state it out loud, so we quietly assume it is easy, or that the files already contain it. They do not.

The hardest and last part of all this is taste. Capability gets a person to competent. You can interview your way to competent fairly directly, because competence lives in procedures and rules and the answers to "what do you do when." Taste is what carries someone from competent to good, and it is much harder to pin down, because it shows up less in conclusions than in what a person chooses to pay attention to in the first place. Two people can reach the same answer, and only one of them was looking at the right things on the way there.

I first borrowed the concept of taste from Ken Kocienda's *Creative Selection*. Kocienda is the engineer who designed the original iPhone keyboard, and his account of taste was precise in a way that named something I had felt about mastery for a long time without having the word for it. Taste is the most expensive thing to elicit from an expert and the most valuable thing to capture, and it is exactly the thing an upload box never reaches. A folder of files does not contain a person's taste. A good interview, asked patiently and in the right order, can begin to draw it out.

There is a milestone I am watching for. Right now, building a real agent that holds someone's expertise is still hard, still a project. I will know the tools have arrived when building one is as easy and low-friction as recording a short video. As simple as filming a TikTok, or simpler than a business person using Excel. That is the product moment worth aiming at: not a more powerful model in the abstract, but the point where the act of getting yourself into an agent costs about as much effort as the most ordinary thing you already do on your phone. When that day comes, the people with knowledge worth capturing will actually capture it, because the price of doing so will have dropped below the threshold where they talk themselves out of it.

This is the thinking behind Foundry, the teach flow inside the marketplace I am building at Valence. Experts do not upload files. An interviewer asks the questions, and the answers fill a structured rulebook, which compiles into a reusable skill that then gets tested rather than trusted on faith. The shape of it follows directly from everything above. We do not ask people to describe themselves, because they are bad at it. We ask them questions, because they are good at answering. We try to capture taste and not only procedure, because procedure alone only ever reaches competent. And we keep pushing the friction down, toward the day when teaching an agent what you know feels less like writing a book and more like pressing record.

Interview, don't upload. The knowledge is already there. The work is in asking for it the right way.
