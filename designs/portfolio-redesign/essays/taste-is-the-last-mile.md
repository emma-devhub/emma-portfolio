# Taste Is the Last Mile

*Why the bottleneck in building great agents is not the model, it is getting what's in an expert's head out of it.*

The default story about agents is that we are waiting on capability. A better model, a longer context window, one more reasoning trick, and then the agent will finally be good enough. I do not think that is where the constraint lives. I have spent the last stretch building agents alongside real domain experts, and the wall I keep hitting is not what the model can do. It is how little of what the expert actually knows ever makes it into the system in the first place.

Capability is increasingly a commodity. What is scarce is distillation: taking a person's tacit method and taste and encoding it so an agent can reproduce their judgment, not just their vocabulary. Right now we do that part badly. We hand someone a text box, ask them to "describe your process," and get back a tidy paragraph that has almost nothing to do with how they actually work. The result is an agent that sounds like the expert and decides like a stranger.

Here is the uncomfortable thing I learned. Most people cannot accurately describe what they know. Ask a senior analyst to write down their method and you get platitudes. The real method is buried in a thousand small calls they make without narrating: which number they check first, what makes them distrust a clean-looking chart, when they stop researching and commit. None of that survives a blank page. But the same person is excellent at answering a specific question. Put a concrete situation in front of them and the judgment comes back instantly and precisely.

That asymmetry is the whole design insight, and it is why a file upload is the wrong interface. You are asking for the one thing humans are worst at: self-description. The right interface is an interview. In the teach flow I built inside our marketplace, which we call the Foundry, an interviewer agent asks one focused question at a time and uses the answers to fill a structured rulebook: the happy path, the decision rules, the anti-patterns, the edge cases, the output format, the tone. The expert never has to know they have a "method." They just have to answer, which is the thing they are already good at. The structure is our job, not theirs. At the end, that rulebook compiles into a reusable skill, and the loop closes: teach, test, publish.

Testing matters as much as teaching, because the interview is not a transcript, it is a probe. You ask, the expert answers, you generate a case the answer did not cover, and you ask again. That is how you surface the edge cases an expert handles on instinct and would never think to mention. Each round narrows the gap between what the agent does and what the expert would have done. You are not collecting their words. You are triangulating their judgment.

Which brings me to taste, the part I am most interested in and the part that resists all of this the longest. Capability gets you to competent. Taste is the last mile to good. And taste is hard to transfer precisely because it does not look like a rule. It lives in the judgment calls, and even more in what a person chooses to pay attention to in the first place. My own taste, the thing I would actually want to pass on, shows up less in my conclusions than in what information I decide is worth taking in and what I ignore. Two analysts with identical models and identical data will produce different work because they attend to different things. That selection, the act of attention before any reasoning happens, is the most expensive thing to elicit and the most valuable thing to capture. A good interview can reach it. A file upload never will.

I came to product through a markets desk, building trading tools, and that work taught me to be suspicious of anything that mistakes output for understanding. A model that produces the right answer for the wrong reasons will eventually produce the wrong answer with total confidence. The same is true of agents. If you have not actually captured the expert's judgment, you have built a convincing impersonation, and impersonations fail exactly when the situation gets interesting.

So here is the milestone I am watching for, and it is not a benchmark score. The day building a real agent is as easy as making a short video, that is the real product moment. Right now, capturing an expert is a project. It should feel like recording a TikTok: low friction, fast, almost fun, with the hard structural work hidden behind a few good questions. Whoever builds that elicitation interface, the one that gets tacit knowledge and taste out of a human without making them write an essay about themselves, does not just ship a feature. They unlock the agent economy, because they unlock the supply of experts willing to be in it. The models will keep getting better on their own. The interview is the part somebody actually has to build.
